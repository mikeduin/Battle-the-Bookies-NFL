What's the idea here?

Battle the Bookies allows you to test your football handicapping skills against both the oddsmakers in Vegas and your fellow game entrants by requiring you to make a single, theoretical $100 bet on every game of the NFL season. Again: that's a fake $100 bet, used just as a vehicle for scorekeeping ... no need for a $10k bankroll to play this game! Your $100 bet on each game can be made in one of three ways:
On the point spread, which are the odds of a team winning or losing by a certain number of points. If a team is favored by 6.5 points, they must win the game by at least seven points to cover the spread. If a team is a 6.5-point underdog, they will cover the spread as long as they do not lose the game by seven points or more.
On a team's moneyline, which is a team's odds of winning the game outright.
On the number of total combined points scored by both teams, on which an entrant can bet either over or under.

The odds for a game will be posted on the Make Picks page as soon as they are set by real-life oddsmakers, and they will change leading up to a game's start time anytime they change in real life. Rather than offering a static, rigid line at a random point prior to the game, Battle the Bookies simulates a real-life handicapping environment by allowing an entrant to "lock in" a pick at any time from when a line is first posted to when the game begins. Once a pick has been submitted, it cannot be changed (otherwise, an entrant would be able to switch their pick and obtain a better line any time the real-life odds moved in their favor).

So I make a lot of fake bets on football games. Then what?

If you win your bet, your game score will be credited with the payout amount that was indicated at the time your bet was placed; if you lose your bet, your score will be a hit with a -$100 loss. Note that a missed or otherwise unsubmitted pick will count as an automatic -$100 loss; if this were not the case, entrants who built up a lead could simply stop picking games until/unless they were caught by their opponents.
From there, you can go in all kinds of fun directions:
The results page shows complete daily picks and results for all entrants. Individual game results and entrants' scores will update on the completion of each NFL game. The results table can be sorted both by year-to-date and daily scores, expressed in terms of profit.
The standings page details a year-to-date breakout of each entrant's performance, expressed as both total profit and wins vs. losses. You can also use the page buttons to scroll through each day's results on the right-hand side of the table.
The user profile pages include detailed analytics of a user's pick tendencies and performance, including statistics for pick types, favorites vs. underdogs, and rolling YTD and daily profit charts.

Tell me about some of this game's awesome, one-of-a-kind features.

GAME ANALYSIS AND CHARTING PAGES: Clicking a game's chart button will take you to that game's analysis page, which includes charts for the three pick types -- Spreads, Moneylines, and Totals -- that detail line movement over the course of the week and highlight when each user's pick was made. Picks are charted based on relative line value to the best line that was ever available for each pick. Click the image to the right to zoom in on an example, or click the green chart button on the left to see a full Game Analysis page in all its glory.
Also included in a game's analysis page are tables showing the best lines ever available -- including applicable juice -- for each of a game's six pick options, as well as a scrollable section ranking each of the pool's entrants based on their pick's capperGrade for that game. But wait -- what the heck is a capperGrade?!
CAPPERGRADES: Forget whether your picks are winning or losing; how good are you at getting the best line available for your chosen selection? This is what capperGrades -- a unique, algorithm-driven evaluation of an entrant's ability to predict line movement -- set out to determine.
Each pick starts with a base capperGrade of 10. For Spread and Total picks, a pick's capperGrade is reduced by the difference between an entrant's selection and the best number ever available for that pick type. From there, a similar calculation is made based on the juice on your pick and the best juice ever available for your selection, and your CapperGrade is reduced by 0.1 for every five cents of difference in juice. capperGrades: Spreads and Totals
The calculations for Moneyline picks are a bit simpler: a pick's starting capperGrade of 10 is reduced by 0.5 for every 20 points in difference between that pick's moneyline and the best moneyline ever available for that selection. This is admittedly a bit inexact, but after running some numbers, 20 points of moneyline movement seemed roughly equivalent to 0.5 points of spread movement.
